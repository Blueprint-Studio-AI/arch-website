Four rules we couldn't break.

Real finance on Bitcoin needs all four at once. Every prior attempt broke at least one — so the markets never came, and Bitcoin's liquidity went everywhere except Bitcoin. Arch is the first to hold all four.

Native

If it isn't your real Bitcoin, you've already lost. So it always is.

Fast

Markets can't form on slow, uncertain recovery. So execution is sub-second.

Decentralized

One custodian is one point of failure. So no single party can move your coin — the validators sign together.

Programmable

Payments alone don't make a market. So Arch runs real programs on native Bitcoin.

Native Bitcoin, made programmable — end to end.

Each layer inherits the one beneath it: a real coin, a chain that speaks Bitcoin, keys split across the validators, and settlement on Bitcoin itself.

It never stops being Bitcoin.

Most "Bitcoin" products hand you a receipt for coins held somewhere else. Arch never does — what you hold is the coin itself, recorded on Bitcoin's own ledger.

  • A real UTXO — not an IOU or a wrapped token
  • Moves settle in a Bitcoin block, like any payment
  • The same coin before, during, and after

A chain that speaks Bitcoin natively.

Apps fire transactions; Arch sequences them into blocks every 180 ms and executes them against real Bitcoin UTXOs. It even has machinery for Bitcoin reorgs — so its record stays consistent with Bitcoin itself.

  • Blocks every 180 ms, run on real UTXOs
  • A UTXO-aware virtual machine
  • Reorg-safe — rolls back and reapplies with Bitcoin

There's no single key to hold.

If Arch can move your Bitcoin, who holds the key? Nobody. The authority to move a coin lives only as shares split across Arch's validators. No single party, Arch included, can move anything alone. On-chain it looks like an ordinary Bitcoin spend, because that's what it is.

  • Authority split across validators (FROST · ROAST)
  • No single party — not even Arch — moves a coin alone
  • On-chain it's an ordinary Taproot spend

Fast where it needs to be. Final where it counts.

Developers can choose when to stay fast on Arch or make things final on Bitcoin. Native Bitcoin, moving faster than Bitcoin alone ever could, then settling with Bitcoin's own finality.

  • Stay fast on Arch, or finalize on Bitcoin
  • Native BTC, faster than Bitcoin alone
  • Settled with Bitcoin's own finality

The financial primitives Arch unlocks.

Pools, enforceable collateral, and real liquidations — the building blocks of markets, finally possible on native Bitcoin.

Bitcoin could send value. It couldn't pool it.

A pool is shared capital many people draw on at once — the basis of almost every market. Bitcoin can't form one alone; Arch can, while every coin stays native.

  • Shared liquidity from native UTXOs
  • The basis for AMMs and efficient markets
  • A market anyone can trade against

Collateral you can actually enforce.

A loan market only works if the lender can seize collateral without the borrower's consent. Arch enforces it mechanically and settles to Bitcoin — the borrower can't yank it back.

  • Seized by rule, not by permission
  • Moving the coin needs the validators' signature
  • Settles on Bitcoin — no wrapping, no bridge

A liquidation that's on time and final.

A late liquidation isn't slow — it's a loss. Arch detects a breach, locks the price at signing, and broadcasts the closeout in under three seconds, then settles on Bitcoin.

  • Price locked at the moment of signing
  • Closeout broadcast in under three seconds
  • Final with Bitcoin's own settlement

Lending on Bitcoin — natively, for the first time.

Native settlement made the collateral enforceable; speed made the liquidations real. Together they make pooled lending possible on Bitcoin itself — no wrapping, no bridge, no custodian.

  • Pools + enforceable collateral + fast liquidation
  • Pooled lending on native Bitcoin
  • Wasn't possible before — it is now
